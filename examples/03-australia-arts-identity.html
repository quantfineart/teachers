Weeks 1-2: Self-Portrait Exploration

4 sessions × 100 min | Focus: Making + Responding

Artist Study: Frida Kahlo & Del Kathryn Barton

How do these artists represent identity? Kahlo's use of symbolism (animals, plants, pain). Barton's hyper-detailed, fantastical self-portraits. Students analyse and discuss: what do these portraits tell us about the artist beyond what they look like?

Session 1: Introduction + Experimental Sketching

  • Class discussion: "What is identity? What makes you, you?"
  • Analyse Kahlo and Barton — visual analysis worksheet
  • Experimental self-portraits: 4 quick sketches (10 min each) using different media — pencil, charcoal, collage, ink
  • Folio entry: annotate sketches with reflections

Session 2: Photography + Mixed Media

  • Self-portrait photography session (school devices or printed photos from home)
  • Mixed media experimentation: layer drawing over photographs, add collage elements, experiment with colour
  • Technique demo: image transfer using gel medium

Session 3-4: Developing the Self-Portrait

  • Choose strongest approach from experiments
  • Develop into a larger, more resolved self-portrait
  • Studio time with teacher circulating for 1:1 feedback
  • End of session 4: peer critique — "Two stars and a wish"

Materials

  • Cartridge paper A3, charcoal, ink, pencils, collage materials
  • Gel medium for image transfer
  • Magazines for collage, scissors, glue
  • Printed photographs (students bring from home or take in class)

Weeks 3-4: Place and Belonging

4 sessions × 100 min | Focus: Making + Responding

Cultural Protocol — Indigenous Australian Art

This unit explores how place shapes identity, including Indigenous Australian connections to Country. Important protocols to observe:

  • Acknowledgement of Country at the start of this unit
  • Do NOT use dot painting as a classroom activity. Dot painting carries specific cultural meanings and belongs to particular Aboriginal nations. Using it as a generic technique is cultural appropriation.
  • Focus on concept, not imitation: explore how Indigenous artists express connection to Country through their own lens — colour, symbol, pattern, story. Students then express their own connection to their own place using their own visual language.
  • Consult with local Indigenous community if possible. Many communities have education liaison officers who can advise or visit.
  • Suggested artists: Emily Kame Kngwarreye, Lin Onus, Reko Rennie (urban Indigenous art challenges assumptions about what Aboriginal art "should" look like)

Artist Study: Emily Kame Kngwarreye, Reko Rennie & a non-Indigenous landscape artist

Compare how different artists represent their connection to place. Kngwarreye's immersive relationship with Country. Rennie's challenge to expectations. A non-Indigenous artist's landscape work (e.g., Fred Williams, John Olsen). What's similar? What's different? Why does perspective matter?

Session 5: Exploring Place

  • Acknowledgement of Country
  • Discussion: "What does 'place' mean to you? Where do you feel you belong?"
  • Artist study: analyse Indigenous and non-Indigenous approaches to place
  • Site sketching: go outside and draw the school grounds / local area. Focus on what makes this place unique.

Session 6-7: Personal Place Artwork

  • Students develop artwork representing THEIR place — could be their suburb, their home, a place they feel connected to, a cultural homeland they've never visited
  • Media: student choice (painting, drawing, collage, printmaking, photography, mixed)
  • Focus on symbolism: what colours, shapes, and images represent this place for you?
  • Folio work: document process, artist references, reflections

Session 8: Responding + Reflection

  • Gallery walk of in-progress work
  • Written reflection: "How does my artwork express my connection to this place?"
  • Prepare folio for Week 5 submission

Materials

  • Painting supplies: acrylic, watercolour, canvas/board
  • Drawing materials, collage supplies
  • Sketchbooks for site drawing

Week 5: Folio Submission 1

Assessment checkpoint

Session 9: Folio Preparation

  • Students compile and organise process folio: research pages, experimental work, annotated sketches, artist studies, reflections
  • Self-assessment against rubric criteria before submission

Session 10: Introduction to Printmaking

  • Technique demo: linocut basics — carving, inking, printing
  • Safety briefing: lino cutter handling, cutting AWAY from the body, bench hooks
  • Students begin practice cuts on scrap lino
Safety: Lino cutters are sharp. Demonstrate safe handling. All students must use bench hooks. No cutting toward hands or body. First aid kit accessible. Students who have not heard the safety briefing may not use the tools.

Weeks 6-7: Printmaking — Layers of Identity

4 sessions × 100 min | Focus: Making

Session 11-12: Design + Carving

  • Design a linocut print that combines self-portrait and place elements — "layers of identity"
  • Concept: printing in 2-3 colours (reduction technique or multiple blocks)
  • Carve design into lino (allow 2 full sessions for carving — it takes time)

Session 13-14: Printing

  • Inking and printing — edition of 3-5 prints
  • Experiment with colour combinations, paper types
  • Folio: document printing process, colour decisions, successes and failures
  • Select best print for final exhibition

Materials

  • Lino sheets (pre-cut to A4), lino cutters (class set), bench hooks
  • Printing ink (water-based), rollers/brayers, printing paper
  • Tracing paper for design transfer
ORDER BY WEEK 3: Lino sheets and printing ink (2-week delivery typical)

Weeks 8-9: Final Artwork Development

4 sessions × 100 min | Focus: Making + Responding

Sessions 15-18: Student-Directed Studio Time

  • Students choose their final artwork medium and approach — can build on any work from the term
  • Must clearly connect to the theme "Identity and Place"
  • Teacher circulates for 1:1 conferences
  • Draft artist statement (150-200 words): what does the work mean? What influenced it? How does it express identity?
  • Peer feedback sessions built in

Week 10: Final Artwork + Artist Statement

Assessment submission

Session 19: Finishing + Statement

  • Final studio time to resolve artwork
  • Finalize artist statement
  • Mount/present work for exhibition

Session 20: Class Exhibition + Reflection

  • Gallery-style exhibition in the art room
  • Students walk through and read each other's artist statements
  • Written reflection: "How has my understanding of identity and place changed through making art?"
  • Celebration of the term's work

Assessment Framework (ACARA)

Two formal assessment points: Folio (Week 5, 40%) and Final Artwork + Artist Statement (Week 10, 60%)

Experimentation & Risk-Taking

Willingness to try new media, techniques, and ideas. Evidence of exploration beyond the comfortable.

Conceptual Depth

Strength of connection between artwork and theme. Thoughtfulness of artist statement.

Technical Skill

Control of materials and techniques. Quality of finished work and presentation.

Reflection & Analysis

Quality of folio documentation. Ability to analyse own and others' work. Written reflections.
