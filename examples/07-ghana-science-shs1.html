Designed for Your Reality

This plan is built around what you actually have — not what a textbook assumes you have.

  • All activities work with everyday materials — most are free or very cheap
  • Activities are designed for groups of 6-7 (manageable with 52 students, one teacher)
  • Peer and self-assessment used throughout to reduce your marking load
  • Lab equipment is marked as optional — there's always a no-lab alternative
  • Estimated prep time: ~30 minutes per week

Week 1: Living and Non-Living Things

6 periods | Content Standard: S1.1.1 — Demonstrate understanding of living and non-living things
Learning Indicators:
  • Classify materials into living and non-living things
  • State the characteristics of living things (MRS GREN)
  • Describe differences between plants and animals

Period 1-2: Introduction and Classification

Activity: Outdoor walk around school compound. Students collect 10 items each (leaf, stone, insect, bottle cap, feather, seed, etc.) and sort into living/non-living on a large chart paper.

Grouping: 8 groups of 6-7 students. Each group gets one chart paper. Discuss within groups first, then share with the class.
No lab needed — the school compound IS the lab for this activity.

Resources: Chart paper (8 sheets), markers, items from the compound

Period 3-4: Characteristics of Living Things

Activity: MRS GREN mnemonic — Movement, Respiration, Sensitivity, Growth, Reproduction, Excretion, Nutrition.

Each group creates a poster for ONE characteristic using local examples:

  • Movement: "How does a grasshopper move differently from a mango tree?"
  • Growth: Students measure their own height, compare with childhood photos brought from home
  • Nutrition: "What do you eat? What does a plant 'eat'?"
  • Reproduction: Cocoa pods, mango seeds, chicken eggs — real local examples
Ghana connection: Use examples from students' own environment — cocoa farming, fishing, market produce. Make science feel like THEIR world, not a textbook world.

Resources: Chart paper, markers, rulers (for height measurement)

Period 5-6: Plants vs Animals

Activity: Venn diagram on the blackboard. Teacher draws, each row nominates one similarity or difference. Students copy into exercise books.

Discussion-based, not lecture-based. Ask: "Can plants move? Can animals make their own food? Why not?"

Quick quiz: 10 multiple-choice questions. Students swap papers and peer-mark. Saves you marking 52 papers.
Homework: Draw and label 5 living things in your community, stating one characteristic of life each one shows.

Week 2: Cells — The Basic Unit of Life

6 periods | Content Standard: S1.1.2 — Demonstrate knowledge of cells
Learning Indicators:
  • Describe the cell as the basic unit of life
  • Identify parts of plant and animal cells
  • State functions of cell organelles

Period 1-2: What is a Cell?

Activity: Analogy — "A cell is like a school."

  • Cell membrane = school fence (controls what goes in and out)
  • Nucleus = headteacher's office (controls everything)
  • Mitochondria = kitchen/canteen (provides energy)
  • Cytoplasm = the school grounds (where everything happens)
  • Vacuole = the storeroom (stores materials)

Students draw the analogy on chart paper — school diagram next to cell diagram.

If microscopes available: Prepare onion cell slides — students observe and draw. If not: Use textbook diagrams or hand-drawn enlargements on the board. Both teach the same concept.

Resources: Chart paper, coloured pencils, textbook diagrams

Period 3-4: Plant vs Animal Cells

Activity: Students draw and label both cells side by side on A4 paper. "Spot the difference" format — circle what plant cells have that animal cells don't (cell wall, chloroplasts, large central vacuole).

Gallery walk — display drawings on the walls. Students walk around and peer-review using sticky notes or verbal comments.

Resources: A4 paper, coloured pencils, Blu-Tack or tape for display

Period 5-6: Functions of Cell Organelles

Activity: "Cell Organelle Job Fair" — each group is assigned one organelle. They create a 2-minute "job description" presentation:

"I am the mitochondria. My job is to provide energy for the cell. Without me, nothing works! I break down food molecules and turn them into energy the cell can use."

Peer assessment: Simple rubric — content accuracy (out of 3), clarity (out of 3), teamwork (out of 3). Each group rates the other groups. Saves teacher marking time.
Homework: Textbook exercises on cell structure. Draw and label a plant cell from memory.

Week 6: Acids, Bases and Salts — pH and Indicators

6 periods | Content Standard: S1.3.1 — Demonstrate understanding of acids, bases and salts
Learning Indicators:
  • Describe the concept of pH
  • Classify substances as acidic, basic, or neutral
  • Use indicators to test pH

Period 1-2: What are Acids and Bases?

Activity: Start with what students already know — "Have you tasted lemon? That sour taste is acid. Have you touched soap? That slippery feeling is a base."

Brainstorm: list all acidic and basic things students can think of from daily life. Create a class chart.

Introduce pH scale (0-14). Draw on board. Students copy into exercise books.

Period 3-4: The Hibiscus Indicator Practical

This is the highlight of the term.

Preparation: Collect hibiscus flowers (roselle/sobolo flowers work perfectly — they grow everywhere in Ghana). Crush petals in water to make a purple-red indicator solution.

Activity: Students test household substances with the hibiscus indicator:

  • Lemon juice — turns pink/red (acid)
  • Vinegar — turns pink (acid)
  • Soap solution — turns green/yellow (base)
  • Wood ash water — turns green (base)
  • Salt water — stays purple (neutral)
  • Coca-Cola — turns red (acid — students love this one)
No litmus paper needed. Hibiscus is free, grows locally, and gives beautiful colour changes. Students can even take the indicator home and test more things.
Grouping: 8 groups. Each group gets a small cup of indicator and 6 test substances in bottle caps or small containers. Teacher prepares these before the lesson (~20 min prep).

Students record results in a table: substance, colour change, acid/base/neutral.

Assessment: Practical assessment — observe students during the activity. Mark: follows instructions, records accurately, can explain results. Quick checklist, not a full written mark.

Period 5-6: pH in Real Life

Activity: Discussion — where does pH matter in Ghana?

  • Farming: Soil pH affects crop growth. Cocoa needs pH 6-7. How do farmers check this?
  • Cooking: Kenkey fermentation is an acid-base process. Banku preparation changes pH.
  • Water quality: Safe drinking water should be pH 6.5-8.5
  • Health: Stomach acid, antacid tablets, heartburn
Ghana connection: This is chemistry students eat, drink, and farm with every day. Make them see it. "Your grandmother who makes the best kenkey? She's a chemist."
Homework: Test 3 things at home with leftover hibiscus indicator. Draw and label the results. Bring to class next lesson.

Remaining Weeks (condensed)

Full detail available for each week — this overview shows the flow.

Strand 1 continued
Wk 3 Levels of organisation. Hands-on: sequence picture cards (cells → tissues → organs → systems) using the human body. Group poster activity.
Wk 4 Properties of materials. Sorting activity with real objects — classify by hardness, flexibility, transparency. No lab needed.
Wk 5 Separation techniques. Practical with everyday items: salt/sand separation using water + cloth filter, rice/stones by hand-picking, oil/water demonstration with cooking oil.
Wk 7 Neutralisation and everyday applications. Antacid demo (bicarbonate + vinegar). Making soap discussion. Extension: local soap-making industry.
Strand 2: Tools and Methods of Science
Wk 8-9 Measurement and SI units. Practical stations: measure length (ruler), mass (kitchen scale or balance), volume (measuring cup/cylinder), time (stopwatch/phone). Estimation before measurement.
Wk 10-11 Scientific method. Students design and carry out their own investigation: "Which type of soil holds the most water?" using school garden soil. Full cycle: observe → hypothesise → experiment → analyse → conclude.
Wk 12 Science and Ghanaian industry — mining (gold, bauxite), cocoa processing, textile production (kente weaving uses dye chemistry), fishing (preservation methods). Students research one industry and present.

Week 13: Revision and End-of-Term Examination

6 periods

Period 1-2: Student-Led Revision

Each group takes one sub-strand and presents a 5-minute recap to the class. Forces students to review and teach — most effective revision method.

Period 3-4: Past Question Practice

Work through past WASSCE/SHS exam questions relevant to Term 1 content. Discuss exam technique: read questions carefully, show working, manage time.

Period 5-6: End-of-Term Examination

Exam structure per GES guidelines:

Section A — Objectives: 40 marks (multiple choice, 40 questions, 45 minutes)

Section B — Essay/Structured: 60 marks (5 questions, answer 4, 75 minutes)

Resource Summary

Free / Very Cheap (used most weeks)

  • Chart paper, markers, coloured pencils, A4 paper
  • Items from the school compound (stones, leaves, insects, soil, seeds)
  • Household items (salt, sand, lemon, soap, vinegar, cooking oil, Coca-Cola)
  • Hibiscus/roselle flowers (free — grow everywhere)
  • Textbook diagrams (drawn large on board if no photocopier)
  • Exercise books for student notes
  • Wood ash (free — from cooking fires)

Lab Equipment (optional — always has an alternative)

  • Microscope + slides (Week 2 cells — optional, textbook diagrams work)
  • Beakers, filter cloth/paper (Week 5 separation — can use cups + cloth)
  • Measuring instruments (Week 8-9 — can substitute with kitchen scale, ruler, plastic cups with markings)
  • Bunsen burner (Week 5 evaporation — can use charcoal stove or sun evaporation)
Estimated teacher prep time: ~30 minutes per week
Mostly collecting materials and preparing chart paper. Peer/self-assessment reduces marking to ~1 hour/week.
